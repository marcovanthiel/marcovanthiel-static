The evening

Four moments, one common thread.

  1. 19:00

    Reception in the hall

    Welcome drink, canapés from our own kitchen and — castle permitting — a short tour of the original chambers.

  2. 19:30

    Five-course dinner

    A seasonal menu by a renowned regional chef, paired with wines the sommelier will gladly tell you about. Each course comes with a short story — about the ingredient, the castle, or the project we are fundraising for tonight.

  3. 21:00

    Musical interlude

    A live performance — classical or jazz, depending on the chosen venue — between the main course and dessert. A short set, but unforgettable.

  4. 22:00

    Silent auction & closing toast

    Your ticket is your entry; the silent auction is your chance to add another dimension to the evening. From a stay in a holiday home to a private museum tour — bid generously, give graciously.

Who are we doing this for?

The proceeds of the castle dinner go in full to one or more projects that make a difference for women and girls — the heart of the Soroptimist mission, Educate to Lead.

Think of education and language classes for women in vulnerable circumstances, microfinance for female entrepreneurs, or shelter and outreach programmes for victims of domestic violence. The specific cause for this edition will be announced in September 2026, in consultation with the Soroptimist club committee.

Why a castle dinner works

Castle dinners are an established format for fundraising in the Netherlands — Soroptimist, Rotary and Lions clubs have been running them with great success for years. For the curious reader, a few building blocks that recur in our evening too:

Atmosphere as leverage

A castle is not a restaurant; it's a setting that lifts the evening and makes guests feel something special is happening. No coincidence that the best-known fundraising dinners take place in just such venues.

Auction as the tipping point

The silent auction — bids on slips against each lot — is a fixed part of the evening. It punctuates the night, gives sponsors visibility, and accounts for 30–40% of the takings coming not from ticket prices but from generosity.

Story between the courses

Not one long speech at the end, but short moments scattered throughout the evening. The guest eats, hears something, eats on, and knows exactly what they have contributed to by the end — without it ever becoming heavy.
